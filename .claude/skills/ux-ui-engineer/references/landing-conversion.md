# Landing and Conversion

Applies only when the surface is a landing, homepage, product marketing, sales, or campaign page. Treat the page as a conversion product: help the right visitor understand the offer, believe it, and take the next step. Visual design supports the engine; the engine is relevance, clarity, proof, objection handling, and next-step confidence.

## Define the page job

One sentence: "This page should persuade [visitor] to [action] because [reason]." Identify the page type (campaign, SaaS homepage, product landing, pricing, waitlist, developer, enterprise sales) and the visitor stage (unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, product-aware, ready to buy). The page must match the stage.

## Hero clarity

The hero promises something specific in the first viewport: a headline of at most two lines that names the category, audience, and outcome; short subtext; one primary CTA visible without scroll. Make the promise specific before making the page impressive. No vague "AI-powered next-gen" framing as a substitute for category, audience, mechanism, and outcome.

## Section anatomy

Choose sections by buyer stage, not a fixed template: hero, social proof, the value sections that matter for this product, objection handling, and a final CTA. Vary layout families (see [imagery-visuals-and-taste](imagery-visuals-and-taste.md)). Cut any section that does not improve comprehension, confidence, or next-step action.

## Proof and trust

Map each claim to evidence and place the proof near the claim it supports. Use authentic product, team, and customer visuals when trust depends on reality. When testimonials are absent, use other proof (usage, integrations, compliance, specifics) rather than fabricating them. Do not encode fake urgency or unsupported metrics as facts.

## CTA strategy

Choose the label, hierarchy, and commitment level to match the stage; repeat the primary action at natural decision points. One primary action per decision area; secondary paths step down.

## Objections, pricing, forms

Answer the visitor's likely objections where they arise. Match pricing disclosure to the business model: self-serve usually benefits from price clarity; enterprise may need a pricing-path explanation. Keep lead forms to the minimum the stage justifies; more fields cost conversion.

## Navigation

Match navigation to page type: campaign pages may reduce exits; product homepages and enterprise sites usually need full navigation. Do not strip navigation reflexively.

## Quality rules

- Do not treat tweets, templates, or viral frameworks as authority; extract what survives scrutiny.
- Prefer real buyer language from sales calls, support logs, and lost-deal objections.
- Preserve the reasoning behind each section so a builder knows why it exists.

## Gate

- **Inspect**: the offer, the visitor, the stage, the available proof, and the constraints (pricing, compliance, brand).
- **Decide**: the page job sentence, the hero promise, the section map by stage, the proof placement, the CTA hierarchy, and the objection/pricing/form strategy.
- **Record** under gate id `G-landing`: the page-job sentence, the visitor stage, the section map, and the proof/CTA decisions. Mark not-applicable with one reason for any non-marketing surface (most product screens).
